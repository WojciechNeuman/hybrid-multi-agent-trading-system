1. Motivate algorithmic trading — why does this problem matter? Markets generate enormous volumes of data at speeds no human can process, and the cost of poor decisions is immediate and quantifiable. Systematic, data-driven approaches have become dominant in professional trading, yet no single method has proven consistently reliable across market conditions.
2. The limitation of single-method approaches — existing systems tend to specialise: deep learning models capture sequential price patterns but are opaque and brittle outside their training distribution; rule-based systems are interpretable but rigid; RL agents optimise for reward but can overfit to simulated environments. Each sees only part of the picture.
3. The heterogeneity argument — different AI paradigms are suited to different aspects of the market. This is the core intellectual justification for your architecture. Define both terms precisely here: heterogeneous describes the nature of the components, hybrid describes their integration into a unified decision system.
4. The sentiment gap — most existing multi-agent and ensemble trading systems focus exclusively on price and volume data. Financial news, social media, and market sentiment indexes carry signals that are structurally different from OHLCV data and are largely ignored. Frame including sentiment as a first-class agent as a concrete contribution.
5. Research questions — state them explicitly:

Does a heterogeneous ensemble outperform any individual agent on risk-adjusted returns?
Does the sentiment agent add measurable value at a swing-trading horizon?
Does dynamic weight adjustment in the supervisor improve over equal weighting?
