\section{Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:eval_framework}

All agents are evaluated on a unified two-year held-out test period, \textbf{1 June 2024 -- 31 May 2026}. This window was chosen because it spans three structurally distinct market regimes that together provide a comprehensive stress test of any active strategy:

\begin{table}[h!]
    \centering
    \footnotesize
    \caption{Three market regimes within the two-year OOS evaluation period. Each regime presents a distinct challenge for active trading strategies.}
    \label{tab:regimes}
    \begin{tabular}{@{}p{1.5cm}p{2.2cm}p{4.3cm}p{4.3cm}@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Regime} & \textbf{Dates} & \textbf{Market Condition} & \textbf{Strategy Challenge} \\
        \midrule
        Chop  & 1 Jun -- 5 Nov 2024  & Sideways, high noise; pre-election uncertainty with violent range-bound oscillations and no net direction. & Avoid over-trading and fee drag during choppy, low-signal conditions. \\[3pt]
        Bull  & 6 Nov 2024 -- 31 Oct 2025  & Parabolic bull market; post-election price discovery; BTC reached an all-time high above \$126{,}000. & Capture trend momentum; avoid premature stop-outs; compare with buy-and-hold. \\[3pt]
        Bear  & 1 Nov 2025 -- 31 May 2026  & Macro capitulation; risk-off unwinding dropped BTC over 50\% from its all-time high. & Profit or preserve capital through short exposure. \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\vspace{3mm}

The choice of this particular window is itself deliberate, on three counts. It is long enough --- almost two years of hourly data --- to give a statistically meaningful number of trades rather than a handful of lucky ones. It contains three genuinely different market conditions, so a strategy must prove itself in more than one environment. And, just as importantly, it is \textit{not} one of the early Bitcoin periods in which simply buying and holding the coin would have returned several hundred percent: over those years Bitcoin was an exceptional asset whose appreciation would flatter almost any long-biased strategy and make for a dishonest baseline. Evaluating on a recent, mixed window keeps the buy-and-hold benchmark fair.

\vspace{3mm}

The multi-regime structure also tests whether the system's edge is genuine \textit{alpha} --- the ability to navigate all three conditions --- or merely the result of riding a directional trend during the bull sub-period. A strategy that beats buy-and-hold in the bull but also stays positive through the chop and the bear has shown genuine resilience across market conditions. Performance is reported both for the full window and per regime, using total return, annualised Sharpe and Sortino ratios, maximum drawdown, and alpha over the corresponding buy-and-hold segment (the financial metrics are defined in Chapter~\ref{07_Financial_Evaluation}).

\vspace{3mm}

Three observations follow. First, the agents' classification AUCs are \textit{modest} --- roughly $0.50$ to $0.54$ where a scalar AUC is applicable --- and yet several agents post large returns. This gap is itself a finding: at this horizon the realised profit is a mixture of weak directional signal and ATR-bracket risk management, not pure forecasting accuracy. Second, \textit{a high out-of-sample return does not, by itself, qualify a method for the fund}. The predeclared roster is the five agents marked in Table~\ref{tab:agent_leaderboard}; two high-returning standalone strategies, PatchTST ($+77.8\%$) and volatility breakout ($+34.7\%$), are deliberately left out --- PatchTST because it has no measurable forecasting skill (below), volatility breakout because its validation-window risk-adjusted return is negative. Third, PatchTST deserves special comment: it is the strongest learned standalone agent on paper, but its directional AUC is $0.50$ --- indistinguishable from chance --- and its $+77.8\%$ is almost entirely directional \textit{beta} from a fixed short-only stance during the bear leg, not forecasting skill. Retraining it walk-forward and adding RevIN instance normalisation (two further attempts) left the OOS AUC at $0.48$--$0.50$, and selecting its trading parameters honestly on validation data turns the $+77.8\%$ into a loss. It is therefore reported as an honest negative rather than included.

\paragraph{Each agent has its own directional mandate.}
A detail that matters for the diversification argument is that the surviving agents are not all trading the same direction. Each one's permitted side is fixed on the validation window --- through an explicit \texttt{trade\_direction} setting and its probability thresholds --- and then frozen, so the realised out-of-sample trades span several tilts. Mamba is explicitly \textbf{long-only} (345 long entries, no shorts), while LightGBM is technically allowed to trade both sides but its frozen thresholds realise only long entries over this hold-out (371 longs, no shorts). The TCN trades \textbf{both sides} and is close to balanced (216 long / 240 short); the trend follower leans short over this window (142 / 395); and the dominance-rotation agent leans long (418 / 281). This spread of mandates is deliberate. Because the roster is rarely all positioned on the same side of the market, the fund's net exposure is set by which views the allocator is currently funding rather than by a single shared directional bet --- the mechanism examined week-by-week in Section~\ref{sec:case_study}.

\paragraph{The LightGBM grid search.}
The gradient-boosting agent is the clearest example of why validation discipline matters. Its final configuration is selected by validation Sharpe over 2022-01--2024-05 with a 120-trade floor, then frozen for OOS. The selected model uses 50 relevance--redundancy features, \texttt{num\_leaves}=31, \texttt{min\_child\_samples}=30, and \texttt{learning\_rate}=0.02. It achieves a validation AUC of 0.553 and an OOS AUC of 0.541, but the trading result compresses to $+9.6\%$ at Sharpe $0.14$ once the market path changes. Figure~\ref{fig:lgbm_importance} shows that the useful features are not a single indicator family: VWAP deviation, short-horizon returns, volume-flow, wick structure, and cross-asset context all contribute small pieces of signal.

\paragraph{Methods that did not make the final system.}
Several additional paradigms were prototyped and honestly rejected. A \textbf{deep reinforcement-learning} agent (PPO over a long/flat/short action space) never developed a positive edge at the hourly horizon: its per-step reward is dominated by noise, and the policy converged only on strongly trending folds while losing through choppy ones. A \textbf{genetic-programming} agent evolved interpretable symbolic rules whose in-sample fitness did not transfer out of sample. An experimental cross-asset learner is not part of the canonical 00--06 artifact set and is omitted. Three further candidates are built but excluded from the predeclared roster: \textbf{PatchTST}, for showing no out-of-sample directional skill (its return is convex-bracket beta, not forecasting), and the \textbf{volatility breakout} and \textbf{contrarian sentiment} (Fear \& Greed) rules, for negative validation-window risk-adjusted performance. Reporting these negatives is part of an honest account: most of what was tried did not make the final system. The roster is a curated, predeclared selection rather than a search over the hold-out; its status as a researcher choice is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\section{Signal Diversity}
\label{sec:signal_diversity}

A multi-agent system can only benefit from combination if its agents are genuinely diverse. Table~\ref{tab:spearman_corr} reports pairwise Spearman rank correlations between the four learned agents' probability signals over the pre-OOS meta-training window. The cross-paradigm pairs are near zero, and even the TCN and PatchTST --- which share an identical 32-feature diet --- correlate at only $0.47$, confirming that their differing inductive biases (local dilated convolution versus global patch attention) produce materially distinct rankings. The rule agents add a further layer of diversity, since their dominance-, breakout-, and trend-based signals are built from logic and, in the dominance agent's case, from an information source no price-feature model uses.

The main improvement is in \textit{risk-adjusted} performance. On total return alone the fund ($+53.7\%$) is below the strongest single agents, especially dominance rotation, and even slightly below the equal-weight fund ($+56.1\%$). But the fund attains the highest \textit{Sharpe ratio} ($1.50$) among the roster agents and benchmarks, with a shallow maximum drawdown ($-9.2\%$), ahead of BTC buy-and-hold (Sharpe $0.09$) and the S\&P~500 (Sharpe $1.16$); the only standalone strategy with a higher Sharpe is the excluded PatchTST, whose return is skill-free bear beta. Diversifying across heterogeneous agents converts volatile individual return streams into a smoother portfolio. The per-regime breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:fund_regime} shows the fund is strongly positive in the bull and bear regimes and flat in chop. To test whether the $1.50$ Sharpe is more than luck, $5{,}000$ random Dirichlet weightings of the same five agents were evaluated: the capped inverse-volatility fund sits at the $87$th percentile of that distribution, but the equal-weight fund already scores $1.48$ --- so the capped allocator adds only a marginal edge, and almost all of the benefit comes from \textit{holding} the diversified roster rather than from the allocation rule. A weekly block bootstrap of the fund's own returns gives a Sharpe $95\%$ confidence interval of $[0.46,\,2.56]$ with $P(\text{Sharpe}>0)=0.99$ --- robustly positive, but with the point estimate in the upper-middle of a wide band.

\subsection{Two Honest Negative Results}
\label{sec:negative_results}

Two negative results are important, because they bound what this thesis can claim.

\paragraph{The adaptive coordinator did not help.}
The regime-gated coordinator --- weights set by a softmax of trailing Sharpe ratios times a per-regime competence score, the ``intelligent'' controller one might expect to be the centrepiece --- returns $-10.2\%$ (Sharpe $-0.23$, drawdown $-32.7\%$), \textit{far worse} on every risk metric than the far simpler capped inverse-volatility rule. This indicates that adaptive coordination did not improve performance: not every component that \textit{looks} agentic and adaptive actually improves a system. The defensible claim of this thesis is therefore hybrid multi-agent \textit{diversification} through a transparent risk-balanced allocator, not that a learned coordinator adds alpha. Indeed the naive equal-weight fund is essentially tied with the capped allocator (Sharpe $1.48$ versus $1.50$); the capped rule is preferred only for its marginally higher Sharpe and its concentration control.

\paragraph{High return alone does not demonstrate forecasting skill.}
The dominance-rotation agent's $+155.1\%$ is the largest single return in the study, and it is tempting to read it as a discovery. It is not. When the agent's signal is replaced by random permutations of itself fed through the same asymmetric ATR bracket, the resulting return distribution is wide, and the agent's true result sits at roughly its $95$th percentile --- inside, not beyond, what an essentially random signal achieves through the same convex risk management. The agent earns its place in the fund only because its return stream is structurally decorrelated from the price-based agents (it reads cross-asset dominance flow), making it a useful \textit{diversifier}; it is explicitly not presented as proven forecasting skill. The same random-bracket discipline is what justified excluding the sentiment and cross-asset experiments. A recurring theme of this work is that, in a domain this noisy, the burden of proof on any ``too good'' result must be high.
