\section{Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:eval_framework}

All agents are evaluated on a unified two-year held-out test period, \textbf{1 June 2024 -- 31 May 2026}. This window was chosen because it spans three structurally distinct market regimes that together provide a comprehensive stress test of any active strategy:

\begin{table}[h!]
    \centering
    \footnotesize
    \caption{Three market regimes within the two-year OOS evaluation period. Each regime presents a distinct challenge for active trading strategies.}
    \label{tab:regimes}
    \begin{tabular}{@{}p{1.5cm}p{2.2cm}p{4.3cm}p{4.3cm}@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Regime} & \textbf{Dates} & \textbf{Market Condition} & \textbf{Strategy Challenge} \\
        \midrule
        Chop  & 1 Jun -- 5 Nov 2024  & Sideways, high noise; pre-election uncertainty with violent range-bound oscillations and no net direction. & Avoid over-trading and fee drag during choppy, low-signal conditions. \\[3pt]
        Bull  & 6 Nov 2024 -- 31 Oct 2025  & Parabolic bull market; post-election price discovery; BTC reached an all-time high above \$126{,}000. & Capture trend momentum; avoid premature stop-outs; compare with buy-and-hold. \\[3pt]
        Bear  & 1 Nov 2025 -- 31 May 2026  & Macro capitulation; risk-off unwinding dropped BTC over 50\% from its all-time high. & Profit or preserve capital through short exposure. \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\vspace{3mm}

The choice of this particular window is itself deliberate, on three counts. It is long enough --- almost two years of hourly data --- to give a statistically meaningful number of trades rather than a handful of lucky ones. It contains three genuinely different market conditions, so a strategy must prove itself in more than one environment. And, just as importantly, it is \textit{not} one of the early Bitcoin periods in which simply buying and holding the coin would have returned several hundred percent: over those years Bitcoin was an exceptional asset whose appreciation would flatter almost any long-biased strategy and make for a dishonest baseline. Evaluating on a recent, mixed window keeps the buy-and-hold benchmark fair.

\vspace{3mm}

The multi-regime structure also tests whether the system's edge is genuine \textit{alpha} --- the ability to navigate all three conditions --- or merely the result of riding a directional trend during the bull sub-period. A strategy that beats buy-and-hold in the bull but also stays positive through the chop and the bear has shown genuine resilience across market conditions. Performance is reported both for the full window and per regime, using total return, annualised Sharpe and Sortino ratios, maximum drawdown, and alpha over the corresponding buy-and-hold segment (the financial metrics are defined in Chapter~\ref{07_Financial_Evaluation}).

\vspace{3mm}

Three observations follow. First, the agents' classification AUCs are \textit{modest} --- roughly $0.50$ to $0.54$ where a scalar AUC is applicable --- and yet several agents post large returns. This gap is itself a finding: at this horizon the realised profit is a mixture of weak directional signal and ATR-bracket risk management, not pure forecasting accuracy. Second, the stricter re-runs changed the individual hierarchy. PatchTST is now the strongest learned standalone agent, Mamba becomes a profitable but drawdown-prone long specialist, and LightGBM contributes only a small positive OOS return after the validation-selected 50-feature search. Third, the TCN no longer receives credit for the earlier long-only bracket result: the canonical version is a both-side, three-class TBM model selected against same-bracket long and short baselines, producing a more modest but cleaner $+36.4\%$ return.

\paragraph{Each agent has its own directional mandate.}
A detail that matters for the diversification argument is that the agents are not all trading the same direction. Each one's permitted side is fixed on the validation window --- through an explicit \texttt{trade\_direction} setting and its probability thresholds --- and then frozen, so the realised out-of-sample trades fall into three groups. Mamba is explicitly \textbf{long-only} (345 long entries, no shorts), while LightGBM is technically allowed to trade both sides but its frozen thresholds realise only long entries over this hold-out (371 longs, no shorts). PatchTST is the opposite --- the only explicitly \textbf{short-only} final agent (130 shorts, no longs) --- and earns its $+77.8\%$ mainly by pressing weakness. The remaining agents trade \textbf{both sides} with characteristic tilts: the TCN is close to balanced (216 long / 240 short), the volatility-breakout agent likewise (238 / 211), the trend follower leans short over this window (142 / 395), and the dominance-rotation agent leans long (418 / 281). This spread of mandates is deliberate. Because the roster is rarely all positioned on the same side of the market, the fund's net exposure is set by which views the allocator is currently funding rather than by a single shared directional bet --- the mechanism examined week-by-week in Section~\ref{sec:case_study}.

\paragraph{The LightGBM grid search.}
The gradient-boosting agent is the clearest example of why validation discipline matters. Its final configuration is selected by validation Sharpe over 2022-01--2024-05 with a 120-trade floor, then frozen for OOS. The selected model uses 50 relevance--redundancy features, \texttt{num\_leaves}=31, \texttt{min\_child\_samples}=30, and \texttt{learning\_rate}=0.02. It achieves a validation AUC of 0.553 and an OOS AUC of 0.541, but the trading result compresses to $+9.6\%$ at Sharpe $0.14$ once the market path changes. Figure~\ref{fig:lgbm_importance} shows that the useful features are not a single indicator family: VWAP deviation, short-horizon returns, volume-flow, wick structure, and cross-asset context all contribute small pieces of signal.

\paragraph{Methods that did not make the final system.}
Several additional paradigms were prototyped and honestly rejected. A \textbf{deep reinforcement-learning} agent (PPO over a long/flat/short action space) never developed a positive edge at the hourly horizon: its per-step reward is dominated by noise, and the policy converged only on strongly trending folds while losing through choppy ones. A \textbf{genetic-programming} agent evolved interpretable symbolic rules whose in-sample fitness did not transfer out of sample. Among the rule agents, a \textbf{contrarian sentiment} (Fear \& Greed) agent returned $-21.1\%$ net of fees, and a \textbf{mean-reversion} agent returned $-12.4\%$; both were excluded. An experimental cross-asset learner is not part of the canonical 00--06 artifact set and is omitted from the final fund. Reporting these negatives is part of an honest account: most of what was tried did not work. Because this inclusion screen references out-of-sample performance, the surviving roster is selected on the hold-out window; the resulting survivorship/selection-on-test bias is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\section{Signal Diversity}
\label{sec:signal_diversity}

A multi-agent system can only benefit from combination if its agents are genuinely diverse. Table~\ref{tab:spearman_corr} reports pairwise Spearman rank correlations between the four learned agents' probability signals over the pre-OOS meta-training window. The cross-paradigm pairs are near zero, and even the TCN and PatchTST --- which share an identical 32-feature diet --- correlate at only $0.47$, confirming that their differing inductive biases (local dilated convolution versus global patch attention) produce materially distinct rankings. The rule agents add a further layer of diversity, since their dominance-, breakout-, and trend-based signals are built from logic and, in the dominance agent's case, from an information source no price-feature model uses.

\subsection{Two Honest Negative Results}
\label{sec:negative_results}

Two findings deserve to be stated plainly rather than buried, because they bound what this thesis can claim.

\paragraph{The adaptive coordinator did not help.}
The regime-gated coordinator --- weights set by a softmax of trailing Sharpe ratios times a per-regime competence score, the ``intelligent'' controller one might expect to be the centrepiece --- returns only $+14.0\%$ (Sharpe $0.33$, drawdown $-18.3\%$), \textit{worse} on every risk metric than the far simpler capped inverse-volatility rule. The lesson is sobering and worth keeping: not every component that \textit{looks} agentic and adaptive actually improves a system. The defensible claim of this thesis is therefore hybrid multi-agent \textit{diversification} through a transparent risk-balanced allocator, not that a learned coordinator adds alpha. The naive equal-weight fund remains highly competitive; the capped allocator is preferred because it gives the strongest Sharpe and a concentration-controlled allocation.

\paragraph{A high return is not the same as alpha.}
The dominance-rotation agent's $+155.1\%$ is the largest single return in the study, and it is tempting to read it as a discovery. It is not. When the agent's signal is replaced by random permutations of itself fed through the same asymmetric ATR bracket, the resulting return distribution is wide, and the agent's true result sits at roughly its $95$th percentile --- inside, not beyond, what an essentially random signal achieves through the same convex risk management. The agent earns its place in the fund only because its return stream is structurally decorrelated from the price-based agents (it reads cross-asset dominance flow), making it a useful \textit{diversifier}; it is explicitly not presented as proven forecasting skill. The same random-bracket discipline is what justified excluding the sentiment and cross-asset experiments. A recurring theme of this work is that, in a domain this noisy, the burden of proof on any ``too good'' result must be high.
